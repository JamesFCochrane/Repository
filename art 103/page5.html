Q1. From the podcast, how has our understanding of what dinoasaurs really look like evolved? Describe the progression of how we portrayed them and how might this effect our understanding of what Archeology is saying about them.

Our understanding of how dinosaurs may look has evolved from viewing them as slow and unintelligent beasts, to seeing them as fast, cunning, and muscular machines. We then began to consider ways in which they may store fat, which we aren't truly aware of due to loss of fat in fossilization. This has made clear to me that we don't actually know what dinosaurs looked like (or sounded like!) and we are all just speculating on it based on skeletal structure. Perhaps we've nailed it, perhaps not. It's fun to imagine the possibilies, and maybe deeper understanding will come from it.

Q3. First, summerize in your own words the definition of 'Material Speculation' as described in the second and fourth article. Then, given the examples in the second article as well as the other examples provided of possible future products, how can 'Material Speculation' also be used to create speculative objects that refer to the past? What might be an example of a fictional object from the past, that creates the same dynamic of speculation.

Material speculation is coming up with objects that would exist in a what-if scenarios of any kind. The objects should in some way reflect in its design the realities of that scenario or world that you are speculating on. Material speculation can be used to imagine what objects may look like if factors of their surroundings or function changed. What would our modern machines look like if we never progressed past steam engines? The possibilities are endless.
