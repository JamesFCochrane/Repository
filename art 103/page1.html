Artist Statement

For these cuts, we made pieces in wood and paper. It was our first time using the laser cutter. My wooden designs are the ones that look kinda like cursive signatures.

I wanted to see how more detailed cuts would turn out. There were some very thin parts, which I really should have taken more caution to avoid moving forward. (I didn't.)

As a group, we all used a common design on 2 sides of each piece that allowed us to fit them together. For our second wood cuts, we cut connecting key pieces from 2 squares of wood.

This is the format we'd use for our final design. We also made some paper cuts, which are the black pieces of paper. Mine are the circles with the 4 equally distanced cuts.

I made them just to see if anything interesting would happen if I linked them together in different ways. They were somewhat interesting but did not lock together well.

Artist Statement

As a group, we approached this assignment with the aim of recreating checkers with a Halloween theme. We wanted to use key fasteners that would connect 2 halfs to make a single board piece while

also serving as the support for player pieces. The board can be assembled in any pattern desired, though edge pieces and corner pieces do face some restriction as far as placement on the board.

We did whatever we wanted as far as shapes for the keys, and something Halloweenish for our player pieces. We modified the way the game was played in a few ways.

We added pieces that don't allow for movement which can be placed by the player, which are the clear pieces. We also modified the rules of the game so that you may move

forwards or backwards at any time and that kinged player pieces are able to move up, down, left, and right, as opposed to just diagonally. For my own pieces, I made the board pieces (the excessively pointy ones)

seemingly as another test to see how far the laser cutter can go. My player pieces were based on things I found cool or funny while also somewhat related to Halloween. I made the alien, minion, and grim reaper.

Things that were successful about the project were the mechanics of the game, the assemblage of the board, and the overall aesthetic. It was fun to play, looked good, and could be assembled in the way we had hoped.

I was also very impressed with my group, the rainbows, who pulled hard throughout and worked some long and stressful hours to put the poject together at all costs. Real troupers. We had some set backs.

Our first pass was made of wood and was 2 layers. After gluing them, we found that the pieces wouldn't be able to fit together on a larger scale. Accounting for the cutting time and painting time that was now delayed,

we decided to move to acrylic instead of wood. We learned that acrylic has a real limit to the amout of detail you can include in your key pieces. Many of mine turned out pretty burnt and some of my board pieces were prone to breakage.

Also, some of the inner slots in the key pieces were unusable due to melting. If I were to do it over, I think some simplification of my key shapes and thickening of lines inside key slots/a new design for player pieces to connect to would be good.
